A media literacy toolkit for recognizing propaganda techniques

Propaganda — is a deliberate form of communication that uses selective, biased, or misleading information to shape people's perceptions and behavior in ways that primarily serve the interests of the communicator, often by appealing to emotion rather than informed, rational judgment.

Example: state leader, authorities, media broadcast channels etc

Gharbzadegi — or "Westoxification" — is a concept used in Iranian political discourse to frame Western cultural influence as a form of spiritual and social contamination. Understanding how this framework shapes media narratives is the first step toward critical thinking in a landscape built on propaganda.

Framework

Three questions to ask
about any content

01
Who made this and why?
Every piece of content has an author with a perspective and an agenda. Knowing who created it helps you understand what they want you to think, feel, or do.
02
Why do I feel scared or angry?
Strong emotional reactions are often intentional. Propaganda is designed to trigger feelings before you have time to think critically about the claim.
03
What do they want me to do?
All persuasive content wants a response — a share, a belief, a vote, or silence. Identifying the intended action reveals the purpose behind the message.

Reference

The four core techniques

⚠️
Fear Appeal

Creates anxiety about an external threat to bypass rational thinking. Effective because fear activates instinct before analysis can occur.

"Your identity is under attack"
👥
Othering

Divides society into us (pure, loyal) and them (infected, foreign). Makes it socially costly to belong to or sympathize with the wrong group.

"Real citizens reject this"
⏳
False Urgency

Adds artificial time pressure to prevent reflection and deliberation. Decisions made under urgency are far easier to manipulate.

"Before it is too late"
🚫
Delegitimization

Attacks the source of an idea rather than the idea itself. Allows dismissal without ever engaging with evidence or argument.

"This is a colonial trick"
